ISSUE #1 — On-Time Delivery Rate shows ~100% for all regions
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Date discovered: May 21, 2026
Where: Report A, Visual 2 (On-Time Rate by Seller Region)

Problem:
  All 5 seller regions show an on-time delivery rate of ~1.0 (100%),
  which produces no meaningful insight and is misleading.

Root cause:
  The ETL computes is_on_time = 1 if order_delivered_customer_date <= order_estimated_delivery_date,
  and is_on_time = 0 if delivered late, and NULL if the order was never delivered.
  The DAX measure excludes NULLs (undelivered orders) from the denominator.
  Since the Olist dataset only records a delivery date for orders that were
  successfully completed, the sample is heavily biased toward fulfilled orders,
  which naturally skew on-time. The undelivered/cancelled orders are silently
  excluded, inflating the rate to near 100%.

Decision:
  Replaced this visual with "Avg Review Score by Seller Region" which shows
  real variation across regions and is a genuinely useful business metric.

Recommendation for documentation (Section C / ETL notes):
  Note that is_on_time should be interpreted with caution — it reflects
  on-time performance only among delivered orders, not all orders placed.

INSIGHT #1 — Revenue & Gross Profit by Seller Tier
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Date discovered: May 21, 2026
Where: Report A, Visual 1 (Revenue & Gross Profit by Seller Tier)

Business Purpose:
  This visual shows how total platform value is distributed across seller tiers.
  It helps identify whether Olist depends mainly on a small group of high-volume
  sellers or on a broad base of medium sellers.

Finding: Silver sellers drive the most total revenue

Observation:
  Silver tier sellers (5K–50K BRL individual revenue) collectively generate
  more total revenue (~8.1M BRL) than Gold sellers (~4.6M BRL) or Bronze (~3.2M BRL).

Interpretation:
  The "middle class" of sellers is the backbone of the platform.
  There are significantly more Silver sellers than Gold, so even though
  each Gold seller individually earns more, the volume of Silver sellers
  dominates the aggregate. This is consistent with the Pareto principle
  applied at the platform level.

Caveat:
  seller_tier is based on cumulative price sum (product revenue, freight excluded),
  not on gross profit or order frequency. A seller with one high-value transaction
  can reach Gold without being a consistently active or profitable seller.
  See Issue #2 below for full documentation of this limitation.

ISSUE #2 — seller_tier and gross_profit: documented limitations
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Date discovered: May 21, 2026
Where: ETL (etl_load_dw.py), dim_seller and fact_order_item

Limitation A — seller_tier is a revenue bracket, not a performance measure
  Definition: Bronze < 5,000 BRL | Silver 5,000–50,000 BRL | Gold >= 50,000 BRL
  Basis: total_revenue = SUM(price) per seller — freight excluded, order count ignored.
  Problem 1: A seller with one expensive sale qualifies as Gold regardless of activity.
  Problem 2: The tier does not reflect margin — a high-revenue, low-margin seller
             ranks the same as a high-revenue, high-margin seller.
  Problem 3: total_revenue for tier uses price only; the revenue field in
             fact_order_item includes freight (price + freight_value). These are
             not on the same basis.

Limitation B — unit_cost is simulated, not from source data
  Definition: unit_cost = list_price * 0.60 (assumes 40% gross margin).
  list_price = avg(transaction price) per product across all orders in dim_product.
  Problem: unit_cost is derived from the product average price, not from the
           specific transaction price. This means gross_profit = price - unit_cost
           uses two different price references. In discounted transactions,
           unit_cost may exceed actual price * 0.60, understating profit.
           In extreme cases, gross_profit can be mathematically negative.

Limitation C — gross_profit excludes freight costs
  revenue = price + freight_value (includes freight as income).
  gross_profit = price - unit_cost (no freight cost deducted).
  These two metrics are not on a consistent basis; gross_profit overstates
  the true margin if freight is a real cost to the seller.

Justification for accepting these limitations (not fixing):
  1. The Olist dataset contains no actual cost data — simulation is the only option.
  2. The assignment explicitly permits "simulated datasets that reflect real-world
     entities and behaviors in a reliable manner" (BIA Project brief, Section B).
  3. The project is described throughout as a "prototype" — not a production
     financial system.
  4. The assignment encourages simplicity: "For simplicity – avoid SCD Type 2",
     "For simplicity – avoid Snowflake". The same principle applies here.
  5. Pre-calculated fact attributes (gross_profit, revenue) are explicitly required
     by the assignment: "if applicable, add pre-calculated fact attributes."

Required disclosure in report (Section C and Part II summary):
  - unit_cost is simulated as avg(transaction_price) * 0.60 per product (SCD Type 1).
  - gross_profit is an approximation and should not be interpreted as an accounting figure.
  - seller_tier reflects cumulative product revenue brackets; it is an operational
    segmentation variable, not a composite performance score.

Business implication:
  Retention and support programs should prioritize Silver sellers —
  losing a significant portion of them would hurt platform revenue more
  than losing a few Gold sellers.

INSIGHT #2 — Review Score vs Revenue per Seller (Scatter Chart)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Date discovered: May 22, 2026
Where: Report A, Visual 3 (Scatter Chart)

Business Purpose:
  This visual shows whether high-revenue sellers also deliver high customer
  satisfaction, or whether revenue and quality are decoupled on the platform.
  It helps identify which individual sellers are assets vs. risks to Olist.

Visual Design Note:
  A logarithmic scale is applied on the Y-axis (Sum of Revenue). This does NOT
  change the data values — a seller with 500K BRL revenue still plots at 500K.
  What changes is the axis spacing: each grid line represents a 10x step
  (0.1K → 1K → 10K → 100K → 1,000K) instead of equal intervals.
  This is necessary because seller revenues span 4 orders of magnitude.
  Without log scale, all Bronze sellers (low revenue) collapse invisibly to
  the bottom, making the chart useless. With log scale, all three tiers are
  clearly visible and comparable.

Findings:
  - Gold sellers (dark blue, large dots) cluster in the top-right — high revenue
    AND high review scores. These are the platform's most valuable sellers.
  - Some Gold sellers appear at review score ~3 — high revenue but mediocre
    satisfaction. These are platform risk sellers: commercially valuable but
    damaging Olist's reputation on partner platforms.
  - Bronze sellers (light blue, small dots) are scattered across the full
    review score range — low revenue, mixed quality. Some have excellent scores
    but haven't scaled. These are growth candidates.
  - Silver sellers (orange) occupy the middle band as expected.

Business implication:
  Olist should not rely on revenue alone to evaluate sellers. A seller in the
  high-revenue / low-review quadrant generates short-term GMV but risks
  degrading Olist's standing on Mercado Livre, B2W, and other partner platforms.
  Quality enforcement must be applied independently of revenue tier.

INSIGHT #3 — Monthly Revenue Trend by Seller Tier (Line Chart)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Date discovered: May 22, 2026
Where: Report A, Visual 5 (Line Chart, Page 2)

Business Purpose:
  This visual shows how revenue evolved over time across seller tiers,
  revealing platform growth trajectory, seasonal patterns, and whether
  each tier is expanding or plateauing.

Findings:
  - All three tiers show consistent growth from Oct 2016 through mid-2018,
    confirming the platform was in a healthy growth phase during this period.
  - Silver sellers consistently lead in revenue volume throughout the period.
  - A visible spike appears around November 2017, likely corresponding to
    Black Friday / seasonal peak demand.
  - Gold and Bronze lines track closely, suggesting similar growth rates
    for the top and bottom revenue brackets during this period.

Data Boundary Note (report disclosure required):
  The sharp decline at the end of the time series (Oct–Nov 2018) reflects
  an incomplete final period in the dataset rather than an actual revenue drop.
  The dataset covers orders through November 2018 with the last months only
  partially populated. This artifact should not be interpreted as a business trend.
